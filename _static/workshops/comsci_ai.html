Warm-up: Act.1

The Mystery Image

09:00 - 9:30 | Practicing Pattern Recognition and Abstraction

Instructions

Participants are given a set of progressively more detailed versions of a blurred or pixelated image.

Starting with the most obscured version, individuals must try to identify the object or scene in the image.

With each new, clearer version, they can refine their guess.

Learning Outcomes

  • Developing pattern recognition skills.
  • Practicing abstraction by focusing on key features, even when details are missing.
  • Understanding how information improves with increasing detail.

Time: 2 minutes per round, 3 rounds total + 4 minutes reflection

Warm-up: Act.2

The Coding Dance

Learning Algorithms Through Movement

Instructions

Assign each participant to a spot. The moderator calls out an instruction, participants follow. Instructions can include

  • Step Forward
  • Turn Left
  • Turn Right
  • Clap
  • Jump

The moderator can combine several instructions as participants get familiar.

Learning Outcomes

  • Understanding the need for precise instructions in algorithms.
  • Learning to follow a sequence of steps.
  • Translating instructions into physical movement.

Time: 3 minutes instruction + 7 minutes execution + 5 minutes reflection

Warm-up: Act.3

The Impossible Shape

Practicing Abstraction and Decomposition

Instructions

Provide participants with a set of tangrams, or pre-cut shapes that can form one square.

Challenge participants to use all seven shapes to create the target object.

  • A Square
  • A rectangle
  • A parallelogram
  • An isosceles triangle

The target shape is shown to participants before the task.

Learning Outcomes

  • Thinking creatively to decompose and abstract
  • Decomposing a complex picture to a series of components
  • Understanding how shapes can be constructed using simpler blocks.

Time: 10 minutes execution + 5 minutes reflection

Where Did Computational Thinking Come From?

A quick look at how the idea entered education

The term was popularized by Jeannette Wing in 2006.

She argued that computational thinking is a fundamental skill for everyone ‚Äî not just computer scientists.

Today, it‚Äôs an essential part of STEAM education worldwide.

It‚Äôs NOT Just Coding!

Computational Thinking is about the way we solve problems ‚Äî not just writing code.

  • It builds logical and creative thinking skills.
  • Coding is one way to apply CT ‚Äî but not the only way!
  • Activities can be unplugged: puzzles, sorting, role play.

What is Artificial Intelligence?

Understanding AI in simple terms.

ü§ñ Definition: AI is when computers or machines are designed to think, learn, and solve problems like humans.

  • AI uses data to make decisions.
  • AI can recognize patterns and learn from experience.
  • AI powers many tools we use every day ‚Äî smartphones, smart assistants, games.

How Does AI Work?

A simple look at what happens behind the scenes.
  • Step 1: AI collects and uses data (pictures, words, numbers).
  • Step 2: It looks for patterns and learns from them.
  • Step 3: It makes decisions or gives suggestions based on what it learned.
  • More data ‚Üí better AI decisions over time.

‚úÖ It‚Äôs like teaching a robot new tricks with practice!

Basic Types of AI

Simple ways to explain different kinds of AI to kids.
  • Reactive AI: Follows simple rules. Example: Chess computer that plays moves.
  • Limited Memory AI: Learns from past data. Example: Self-driving cars remembering signs.
  • Everyday AI: Smart speakers, recommendation systems, chatbots.

‚úÖ Keep it simple ‚Äî focus on examples kids know!

AI and CT go hand in hand!
  • AI uses Decomposition ‚Äî breaking big tasks into smaller steps.
  • It relies on Pattern Recognition ‚Äî finding trends in lots of data.
  • It needs Abstraction ‚Äî focusing on what matters in the data.
  • It follows Algorithms ‚Äî clear instructions for solving problems.
